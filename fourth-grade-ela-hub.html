Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Fourth Grade ELA

Reading Literary Text (ELAGSE4RL)

  • ELAGSE4RL1: Refer to details and examples in a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences from the text
  • ELAGSE4RL2: Determine a theme of a story, drama, or poem from details in the text; summarize the text
  • ELAGSE4RL3: Describe in depth a character, setting, or event in a story or drama, drawing on specific details in the text
  • ELAGSE4RL4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, including those that allude to significant characters found in mythology
  • ELAGSE4RL5: Explain major differences between poems, drama, and prose, and refer to structural elements of poems and drama when writing or speaking
  • ELAGSE4RL6: Compare and contrast the point of view from which different stories are narrated, including the difference between first- and third-person narrations
  • ELAGSE4RL7: Make connections between the text of a story or drama and a visual or oral presentation of the text
  • ELAGSE4RL9: Compare and contrast the treatment of similar themes and topics and patterns of events in stories, myths, and traditional literature from different cultures
  • ELAGSE4RL10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literature, including stories, dramas, and poetry, in the grades 4-5 text complexity band proficiently

Reading Informational Text (ELAGSE4RI)

  • ELAGSE4RI1: Refer to details and examples in a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences from the text
  • ELAGSE4RI2: Determine the main idea of a text and explain how it is supported by key details; summarize the text
  • ELAGSE4RI3: Explain events, procedures, ideas, or concepts in a historical, scientific, or technical text, including what happened and why
  • ELAGSE4RI4: Determine the meaning of general academic and domain-specific words or phrases in a text relevant to a grade 4 topic or subject area
  • ELAGSE4RI5: Describe the overall structure of events, ideas, concepts, or information in a text or part of a text
  • ELAGSE4RI6: Compare and contrast a firsthand and secondhand account of the same event or topic; describe the differences in focus and the information provided
  • ELAGSE4RI7: Interpret information presented visually, orally, or quantitatively and explain how it contributes to understanding
  • ELAGSE4RI8: Explain how an author uses reasons and evidence to support particular points in a text
  • ELAGSE4RI9: Integrate information from two texts on the same topic in order to write or speak about the subject knowledgeably
  • ELAGSE4RI10: By the end of year, read and comprehend informational texts, including history/social studies, science, and technical texts, in the grades 4-5 text complexity band proficiently

Reading Foundational Skills (ELAGSE4RF)

  • ELAGSE4RF3: Know and apply grade-level phonics and word analysis skills in decoding words
  • ELAGSE4RF3a: Use combined knowledge of all letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology to read accurately unfamiliar multisyllabic words
  • ELAGSE4RF4: Read with sufficient accuracy and fluency to support comprehension
  • ELAGSE4RF4a: Read grade-level text with purpose and understanding
  • ELAGSE4RF4b: Read grade-level prose and poetry orally with accuracy, appropriate rate, and expression
  • ELAGSE4RF4c: Use context to confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, rereading as necessary

Writing (ELAGSE4W)

  • ELAGSE4W1: Write opinion pieces on topics or texts, supporting a point of view with reasons and information
  • ELAGSE4W2: Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas and information clearly
  • ELAGSE4W3: Write narratives to develop real or imagined experiences or events using effective technique, descriptive details, and clear event sequences
  • ELAGSE4W4: Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development and organization are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience
  • ELAGSE4W5: With guidance and support from peers and adults, develop and strengthen writing by planning, revising, and editing
  • ELAGSE4W6: With some guidance and support from adults, use technology, including the Internet, to produce and publish writing as well as to interact and collaborate with others

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Fourth Grade Literacy Development

Fourth grade represents the heart of the "reading to learn" phase. Students should have automatic word recognition skills, allowing them to focus cognitive resources on comprehension and analysis. This is the year to build strong literary analysis skills and introduce more complex informational texts across content areas.

Key Focus Areas: Theme identification, text evidence, comparing/contrasting, and integrating information from multiple sources are critical fourth-grade skills that build toward middle school expectations.

Literary Analysis Skills

Theme vs. Main Idea

Students often confuse these concepts:

  • Main Idea: What the text is mostly about (used for informational text)
  • Theme: The life lesson or message the author wants readers to take away (used for literature)

Themes are typically stated as universal statements (e.g., "True friendship requires sacrifice") rather than topics (e.g., "friendship").

Figurative Language

Types to Teach

  • Simile: Compares using "like" or "as" (fast as lightning)
  • Metaphor: Direct comparison without like/as (life is a journey)
  • Idiom: Expression with non-literal meaning (raining cats and dogs)
  • Personification: Giving human qualities to non-human things
  • Hyperbole: Extreme exaggeration for effect
  • Adage/Proverb: Traditional wise sayings

📝 Lesson Ideas and Instructional Strategies

Theme Identification Lesson

Objective

Students will determine the theme of a story by analyzing character actions, events, and the story's resolution.

Theme vs. Topic

First, clarify the difference:

  • Topic: One word or phrase (friendship, courage, family)
  • Theme: A complete statement about that topic (True friends stand by you in difficult times)

Steps to Find Theme

  1. Identify the topic (What is this story about?)
  2. Examine character actions and decisions
  3. Consider the problem and how it was resolved
  4. Ask: What lesson did the character learn?
  5. State the theme as a universal statement
Common Themes: Create an anchor chart of themes found in literature: perseverance, kindness, courage, honesty, friendship, acceptance, cooperation, responsibility.

Comparing Point of View Lesson

Objective

Students will compare and contrast first-person and third-person narration and explain how point of view affects a story.

Mentor Texts

  • First Person: Diary of a Wimpy Kid, Wonder (August's sections)
  • Third Person Limited: Because of Winn-Dixie
  • Third Person Omniscient: Charlotte's Web

Lesson Procedure

  1. Read two passages about the same event from different POVs
  2. Chart what we learn from each narrator
  3. Discuss: What can we know? What can't we know?
  4. Students rewrite a paragraph changing the POV

Greek and Latin Roots Lesson

Objective

Students will use knowledge of Greek and Latin roots to determine the meaning of unfamiliar words.

Weekly Root Study Routine

Day Activity
Monday Introduce root, meaning, and origin. Create word web with examples.
Tuesday Word sort: Identify words with the root in context sentences.
Wednesday Word building: Combine roots with prefixes and suffixes.
Thursday Apply: Find words with the root in texts we're reading.
Friday Assessment: Use roots to determine meaning of new words.

Opinion Writing with Evidence Lesson

Objective

Students will write an opinion piece supporting their point of view with reasons and information from texts.

RACES Paragraph Structure

  • Restate the question in your answer
  • Answer with your opinion
  • Cite evidence from the text
  • Explain how the evidence supports your opinion
  • Sum up your argument

Linking Words

Teach transitional phrases for opinion writing:

  • To introduce opinion: In my opinion, I believe, From my point of view
  • To add reasons: Furthermore, In addition, Another reason
  • To cite evidence: For example, According to the text, The author states
  • To conclude: Therefore, For these reasons, In conclusion

Firsthand vs. Secondhand Accounts

Objective

Students will compare firsthand (primary) and secondhand (secondary) accounts of the same event.

Definitions

  • Firsthand/Primary: Written by someone who was there (diary, letter, autobiography, interview)
  • Secondhand/Secondary: Written by someone who learned about it (textbook, encyclopedia, biography)

Comparison Activity

  1. Read a firsthand account of a historical event (diary entry, letter)
  2. Read a secondhand account of the same event (textbook passage)
  3. Compare: What's included in each? What's the focus? What perspective is shown?
  4. Discuss advantages and limitations of each type of source

🔍 Close Reading and Text Analysis

Text Evidence Investigation

Teaching the "ACE" Strategy

For answering text-dependent questions:

  • Answer the question
  • Cite evidence from the text
  • Explain how the evidence supports your answer

Citing Evidence Stems

  • "According to the text..."
  • "On page __, the author states..."
  • "The text says..."
  • "This is evident when..."
  • "The author writes..."

Myth Comparison Investigation

Essential Question

How do myths from different cultures explain similar phenomena, and what do they reveal about those cultures?

Text Set: Why the Sun and Moon Exist

  • Greek: Apollo and Helios myths
  • Norse: Sol and Mani myths
  • Native American: Various creation stories
  • Egyptian: Ra mythology

Analysis Questions

  1. What do these myths explain?
  2. What characters appear? Are they gods, humans, animals?
  3. What cultural values are reflected?
  4. What patterns appear across cultures?

Character Analysis Investigation

Deep Character Study

Using STEAL method with text evidence:

Element Question Text Evidence Character Trait
Speech What does the character say? [Quote from text] [Inferred trait]
Thoughts What does the character think? [Quote from text] [Inferred trait]
Effect How do others react to the character? [Quote from text] [Inferred trait]
Actions What does the character do? [Quote from text] [Inferred trait]
Looks How is the character described? [Quote from text] [Inferred trait]

Author's Purpose and Perspective

Analyzing Author's Choices

Guide students to analyze why authors make certain choices:

  • Why did the author use this word instead of another?
  • What effect does the author's word choice have on readers?
  • How does the author feel about this topic? What evidence shows this?
  • What reasons and evidence does the author provide?

Bias Detection

Introduce the concept of bias in informational texts:

  • Who wrote this? What might be their perspective?
  • Is this fact or opinion? How can we tell?
  • What information might be missing?
  • How might someone with a different view write about this?

Research Investigation

Multi-Source Research Project

Students investigate a topic using multiple sources:

  1. Wonder: Generate questions about the topic
  2. Find Sources: Locate 2-3 sources (books, websites, articles)
  3. Take Notes: Use graphic organizers to record information
  4. Synthesize: Combine information from multiple sources
  5. Write: Create informative report with evidence from sources
  6. Cite: Create a simple bibliography
Note-Taking Skills: Explicitly teach paraphrasing. Model: "The source says... In my own words, this means..."

❌ Common Misconceptions

Reading Misconceptions

Theme = Topic

Reality: Topic is one word (friendship). Theme is a complete statement about that topic (True friends support each other through difficulties).

Main idea is always in the first sentence

Reality: Main idea can be anywhere in a paragraph or implied across the whole text.

First person always uses "I"

Reality: First person can also use "we" for plural. The key is the narrator is a character in the story.

Writing Misconceptions

Longer = Better

Reality: Quality matters more than quantity. Strong writing is focused and uses precise words.

Good writers don't need to revise

Reality: All writers revise. The best writing comes from multiple drafts.

Grammar Misconceptions

Never start a sentence with "Because"

Reality: You can start with "Because" if you complete the sentence with an independent clause.

Always add -ly to make an adverb

Reality: Some adverbs don't end in -ly (fast, hard, well).

🎓 AI Tools for Students

Guidelines for Student AI Use

Important Considerations:
  • Most AI tools require users to be 13+ (check terms of service)
  • Fourth graders should use AI only under adult supervision
  • Focus on educational AI tools designed for students
  • Teach critical evaluation of AI-generated content
  • Emphasize AI as a learning tool, not a replacement for thinking

Teaching AI Literacy

Fourth Grade Conversations About AI

  • How does AI "learn"? It's trained on information from the internet and books.
  • AI can make mistakes. Always verify information.
  • AI can't replace your own thinking and creativity.
  • Using AI to do your work for you is like having someone else exercise for you—you don't get stronger.
  • Never share personal information with AI tools.
