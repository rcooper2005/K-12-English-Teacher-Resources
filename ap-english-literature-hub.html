📋 AP English Literature Standards

College Board AP English Literature Course Framework

Course Skills

Skill Description
1. Explain the function of character Identify and describe what specific textual details reveal about a character and their perspective
2. Explain the function of setting Explain the function of setting in a narrative
3. Explain the function of plot and structure Explain the function of structure in a text
4. Explain the function of the narrator or speaker Identify and explain the function of point of view
5. Explain the function of word choice, imagery, and symbols Explain the function of specific words and phrases
6. Explain the function of comparison Identify and explain the function of comparison
7. Develop textually substantiated arguments Develop a thesis statement that conveys a defensible interpretation

Big Ideas

  • CHR - Character: Characters in literature allow readers to study and explore human conditions and conflicts
  • SET - Setting: Setting and the details associated with it establish mood and affect characters and plot
  • STR - Structure: The arrangement of parts and sections of a text contributes to meaning
  • NAR - Narration: A narrator's perspective controls the details and emphases that affect how readers experience a text
  • FIG - Figurative Language: Comparisons, representations, and associations create meaning
  • LAN - Literary Argumentation: Readers establish interpretations through arguments about texts

AP Exam Format

Section I: Multiple Choice (45% of score)

  • 55 questions in 60 minutes
  • 4-5 prose fiction and poetry passages

Section II: Free Response (55% of score)

  • Q1 - Poetry Analysis: Analyze a poem examining how literary elements contribute to meaning (suggested 40 minutes)
  • Q2 - Prose Fiction Analysis: Analyze a prose passage examining how literary elements contribute to meaning (suggested 40 minutes)
  • Q3 - Literary Argument: Develop literary argument about a novel, play, or poem from a list or student choice (suggested 40 minutes)

Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - 11th-12th Grade

AP Literature aligns with 11-12 GSE standards with emphasis on literary analysis and interpretation.

Key Standards Alignment

  • ELAGSE11-12RL1: Cite strong and thorough textual evidence including where text leaves matters uncertain
  • ELAGSE11-12RL2: Analyze two or more themes and how they interact
  • ELAGSE11-12RL3: Analyze author's choices regarding how to develop and relate elements
  • ELAGSE11-12RL4: Analyze word choices on meaning and tone, including language that is fresh, engaging, or beautiful
  • ELAGSE11-12RL5: Analyze structure's contribution to meaning and aesthetic impact
  • ELAGSE11-12RL6: Analyze cases requiring distinction between stated and meant (irony, satire)
  • ELAGSE11-12RL9: Demonstrate knowledge of foundational works

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Literary Analysis Approaches

Character Analysis

  • Motivation and desires
  • Internal conflicts
  • Development and change
  • Relationships with other characters
  • Function in advancing theme

Setting Analysis

  • Physical environment
  • Time period and historical context
  • Social/cultural environment
  • Atmosphere and mood
  • Symbolic significance

Structure Analysis

  • Narrative structure (chronology, flashback)
  • Chapter/scene divisions
  • Beginnings and endings
  • Juxtaposition and contrast
  • Pacing and tension

Narration Analysis

  • Point of view (1st, 3rd limited, omniscient)
  • Reliability of narrator
  • Narrative distance
  • Stream of consciousness
  • Multiple narrators

Poetry Analysis

Elements to Analyze

Element Focus
Speaker Who speaks? What is their perspective, tone, situation?
Form/Structure Stanza structure, line breaks, traditional forms (sonnet, villanelle)
Sound Devices Rhyme, rhythm, meter, alliteration, assonance
Imagery Sensory details, visual images, patterns of imagery
Figurative Language Metaphor, simile, personification, symbolism
Diction Word choice, connotation, tone
Syntax Sentence structure, enjambment, caesura

Developing Literary Arguments

The Thesis Statement

A defensible interpretation that:

  • Makes a claim about the text's meaning
  • Can be supported with textual evidence
  • Is not a statement of fact or summary
  • Reveals insight about how the text works

Evidence and Commentary

  • Select specific, relevant textual evidence
  • Explain HOW evidence supports interpretation
  • Connect analysis back to thesis
  • Build a line of reasoning

Critical Lenses

Formalist/New Criticism

Focus on text itself—structure, language, imagery

Feminist

Gender roles, power dynamics, representation

Marxist

Class, economics, power structures

Postcolonial

Colonial power, othering, cultural identity

Psychoanalytic

Unconscious motivations, symbols, desires

Reader Response

Reader's role in creating meaning

Poetry Analysis Lesson Sequence

  2. Day 2: Form and structure
  3. Day 3: Imagery and figurative language
  4. Day 4: Diction and tone

Prose Fiction Analysis

Passage Analysis Protocol

  1. First read for comprehension
  2. Annotate for literary elements
  3. Identify patterns and connections
  4. Develop interpretive thesis
  5. Select and organize evidence
  6. Write analysis essay

Literary Argument (Q3) Preparation

Building a Text Repertoire

  • Read diverse works of literary merit
  • Track themes, characters, and techniques
  • Practice applying different works to prompts
  • Develop flexibility in text selection

🔍 Close Reading and Analysis

Close Reading Protocol

Multi-Read Strategy

  1. First Read: What happens? What is this about?
  2. Second Read: How does it work? (literary elements)
  3. Third Read: Why does it matter? (meaning, theme)
  4. Fourth Read: How do elements work together?

Comparative Analysis

Essential Question

How do different texts/authors approach similar themes or use similar techniques differently?

❌ Common Misconceptions

Literary analysis = summary

Reality: Analysis explains HOW and WHY, not just WHAT happens.

There's one "right" interpretation

Reality: Multiple valid interpretations exist if supported by evidence.

Complex vocabulary = good analysis

Reality: Clear, precise analysis matters more than impressive vocabulary.
