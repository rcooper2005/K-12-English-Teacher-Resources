📋 AP English Language Standards

College Board AP English Language Course Framework

Course Skills

Skill Description
1. Rhetorical Situation Explain how writers' and speakers' choices reflect the components of the rhetorical situation
2. Claims and Evidence Make strategic choices in a text to address a rhetorical situation
3. Reasoning and Organization Explain how the organization of a text creates unity and coherence and reflects a line of reasoning
4. Style Explain how writers' stylistic choices contribute to a text's purpose and meaning

Big Ideas

  • RHS - Rhetorical Situation: Individuals write within a particular situation and make strategic writing choices based on that situation
  • CLE - Claims and Evidence: Writers make claims about subjects, rely on evidence, and choose how to present evidence
  • REO - Reasoning and Organization: Writers guide understanding through claims, reasoning, organization, and evidence
  • STL - Style: Stylistic choices contribute to effectiveness of an argument

AP Exam Format

Section I: Multiple Choice (45% of score)

  • 45 questions in 60 minutes
  • 5 passages with questions testing reading and writing skills

Section II: Free Response (55% of score)

  • Q1 - Synthesis: Read 6-7 sources and synthesize at least 3 to support an argument (suggested 55 minutes)
  • Q2 - Rhetorical Analysis: Analyze how a writer uses rhetorical strategies to achieve purpose (suggested 40 minutes)
  • Q3 - Argument: Develop argument on a given topic using evidence from reading, observation, experience (suggested 40 minutes)

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

The Rhetorical Situation

Components (SOAPS)

Element Questions
Speaker/Writer Who is communicating? What is their background, credibility, perspective?
Occasion What prompted the communication? What is the context?
Audience Who is the intended audience? What are their values, knowledge, expectations?
Purpose What is the writer trying to accomplish?
Subject What is the topic? What is the exigence (urgent need)?

Rhetorical Appeals

Ethos (Credibility)

  • Establishing expertise
  • Demonstrating good character
  • Showing goodwill toward audience
  • Using credible sources

Pathos (Emotion)

  • Appealing to values
  • Creating emotional connection
  • Using vivid language/imagery
  • Telling compelling stories

Logos (Logic)

  • Using evidence and facts
  • Employing logical reasoning
  • Building structured arguments
  • Addressing counterarguments

Argument Structure

Toulmin Model

Element Definition
Claim The main argument or thesis
Evidence/Grounds Data, facts, examples supporting the claim
Warrant The logical connection between evidence and claim
Backing Support for the warrant
Qualifier Words limiting the claim (usually, sometimes)
Rebuttal Acknowledgment of counterarguments

Rhetorical Devices

Device Definition Effect
Anaphora Repetition at beginning of clauses Emphasis, rhythm, unity
Antithesis Contrasting ideas in parallel structure Clarification through contrast
Parallelism Similar grammatical structure Rhythm, clarity, emphasis
Chiasmus Reversed parallel structure (AB:BA) Memorable emphasis
Zeugma One word governing multiple elements Wit, economy
Asyndeton Omission of conjunctions Speed, urgency
Polysyndeton Multiple conjunctions Deliberation, accumulation

Synthesis Essay Skills

Source Integration

  • Develop clear, defensible thesis
  • Synthesize at least 3 sources (not just summarize)
  • Use sources to support YOUR argument
  • Integrate quotations smoothly
  • Cite sources appropriately

📝 Lesson Ideas

Rhetorical Analysis Lesson Sequence

Week-Long Unit

  1. Day 1: Introduce rhetorical situation (SOAPS)
  2. Day 2: Analyze appeals (ethos, pathos, logos)
  3. Day 3: Identify and analyze rhetorical devices
  4. Day 4: Practice analysis with model essays
  5. Day 5: Timed rhetorical analysis practice

Synthesis Essay Lesson

Building Synthesis Skills

  1. Introduce synthesis vs. summary
  2. Practice annotating sources for synthesis
  3. Model thesis development with multiple sources
  4. Practice integrating and citing sources
  5. Peer review and revision
  6. Timed synthesis practice

Argument Essay Lesson

Developing Sophisticated Arguments

  • Analyze AP argument prompts
  • Practice developing defensible claims
  • Build evidence from reading, observation, experience
  • Address counterarguments and concessions
  • Develop varied, sophisticated style

🔍 Close Reading and Analysis

Rhetorical Analysis Investigation

Deep Dive Protocol

  1. Establish rhetorical situation (SOAPS)
  2. Identify writer's purpose and audience
  3. Analyze rhetorical choices (appeals, devices, style)
  4. Evaluate effectiveness
  5. Write analysis connecting choices to purpose

Comparing Rhetorical Strategies

Essential Question

How do different writers use different rhetorical strategies to address similar topics?

❌ Common Misconceptions

Rhetorical analysis = summary

Reality: Analysis focuses on HOW and WHY, not just WHAT.

Synthesis = using sources as quotes

Reality: Synthesis means weaving sources into YOUR argument.
