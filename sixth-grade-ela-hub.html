📋 Sixth Grade ELA Standards

Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Sixth Grade ELA

Reading Literary Text (ELAGSE6RL)

  • ELAGSE6RL1: Cite textual evidence to support analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text
  • ELAGSE6RL2: Determine a theme or central idea of a text and how it is conveyed through particular details; provide a summary of the text distinct from personal opinions or judgments
  • ELAGSE6RL3: Describe how a particular story's or drama's plot unfolds in a series of episodes as well as how the characters respond or change as the plot moves toward a resolution
  • ELAGSE6RL4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, including figurative and connotative meanings; analyze the impact of a specific word choice on meaning and tone
  • ELAGSE6RL5: Analyze how a particular sentence, chapter, scene, or stanza fits into the overall structure of a text and contributes to the development of the theme, setting, or plot
  • ELAGSE6RL6: Explain how an author develops the point of view of the narrator or speaker in a text
  • ELAGSE6RL7: Compare and contrast the experience of reading a story, drama, or poem to listening to or viewing an audio, video, or live version of the text
  • ELAGSE6RL9: Compare and contrast texts in different forms or genres in terms of their approaches to similar themes and topics
  • ELAGSE6RL10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literature, including stories, dramas, and poems, in the grades 6-8 text complexity band proficiently

Reading Informational Text (ELAGSE6RI)

  • ELAGSE6RI1: Cite textual evidence to support analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text
  • ELAGSE6RI2: Determine a central idea of a text and how it is conveyed through particular details; provide a summary of the text distinct from personal opinions or judgments
  • ELAGSE6RI3: Analyze in detail how a key individual, event, or idea is introduced, illustrated, and elaborated in a text
  • ELAGSE6RI4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, including figurative, connotative, and technical meanings
  • ELAGSE6RI5: Analyze how a particular sentence, paragraph, chapter, or section fits into the overall structure of a text and contributes to the development of the ideas
  • ELAGSE6RI6: Determine an author's point of view or purpose in a text and explain how it is conveyed in the text
  • ELAGSE6RI7: Integrate information presented in different media or formats as well as in words to develop a coherent understanding of a topic or issue
  • ELAGSE6RI8: Trace and evaluate the argument and specific claims in a text, distinguishing claims that are supported by reasons and evidence from claims that are not
  • ELAGSE6RI9: Compare and contrast one author's presentation of events with that of another
  • ELAGSE6RI10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literary nonfiction in the grades 6-8 text complexity band proficiently

Writing (ELAGSE6W)

  • ELAGSE6W1: Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence
  • ELAGSE6W2: Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas, concepts, and information through the selection, organization, and analysis of relevant content
  • ELAGSE6W3: Write narratives to develop real or imagined experiences or events using effective technique, relevant descriptive details, and well-structured event sequences
  • ELAGSE6W4: Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience
  • ELAGSE6W5: With some guidance and support from peers and adults, develop and strengthen writing as needed by planning, revising, editing, rewriting, or trying a new approach
  • ELAGSE6W6: Use technology, including the Internet, to produce and publish writing as well as to interact and collaborate with others
  • ELAGSE6W7: Conduct short research projects to answer a question, drawing on several sources and refocusing the inquiry when appropriate
  • ELAGSE6W8: Gather relevant information from multiple print and digital sources; assess the credibility of each source; and quote or paraphrase the data and conclusions of others while avoiding plagiarism and providing basic bibliographic information for sources
  • ELAGSE6W9: Draw evidence from literary or informational texts to support analysis, reflection, and research
  • ELAGSE6W10: Write routinely over extended time frames and shorter time frames for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences

Speaking and Listening (ELAGSE6SL)

  • ELAGSE6SL1: Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions with diverse partners on grade 6 topics, texts, and issues, building on others' ideas and expressing their own clearly
  • ELAGSE6SL2: Interpret information presented in diverse media and formats and explain how it contributes to a topic, text, or issue under study
  • ELAGSE6SL3: Delineate a speaker's argument and specific claims, distinguishing claims that are supported by reasons and evidence from claims that are not
  • ELAGSE6SL4: Present claims and findings, sequencing ideas logically and using pertinent descriptions, facts, and details to accentuate main ideas or themes; use appropriate eye contact, adequate volume, and clear pronunciation
  • ELAGSE6SL5: Include multimedia components and visual displays in presentations to clarify information
  • ELAGSE6SL6: Adapt speech to a variety of contexts and tasks, demonstrating command of formal English when indicated or appropriate

Language (ELAGSE6L)

  • ELAGSE6L1: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English grammar and usage when writing or speaking
  • ELAGSE6L2: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English capitalization, punctuation, and spelling when writing
  • ELAGSE6L3: Use knowledge of language and its conventions when writing, speaking, reading, or listening
  • ELAGSE6L4: Determine or clarify the meaning of unknown and multiple-meaning words and phrases based on grade 6 reading and content
  • ELAGSE6L5: Demonstrate understanding of figurative language, word relationships, and nuances in word meanings
  • ELAGSE6L6: Acquire and use accurately grade-appropriate general academic and domain-specific words and phrases

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

The Middle School Transition

Sixth grade marks a significant transition as students move from elementary to middle school literacy expectations. The focus shifts from "learning to read" to sophisticated "reading to learn" with increased emphasis on analysis, argumentation, and critical thinking. Students are expected to cite textual evidence, analyze author's craft, and produce well-developed arguments.

Key Shifts: Sixth grade introduces formal argument writing (distinct from opinion writing), objective summary (distinct from personal opinions), and analysis of author's purpose and point of view at a deeper level.

Citing Textual Evidence

Teaching Evidence-Based Responses

The standard "cite textual evidence" appears throughout sixth grade. Students must:

  • Select evidence that directly supports their claim
  • Integrate quotes smoothly into their writing
  • Explain HOW the evidence supports their analysis
  • Distinguish between explicit information and inferences

Evidence Integration Stems

  • According to the text, "..." (page #).
  • The author states, "..." which shows that...
  • This is evident when [character/author] says, "..."
  • On page #, the text describes... This suggests...

Objective Summary vs. Personal Response

Teaching Objective Summary

Objective Summary Personal Response
Includes only what the text says Includes opinions and reactions
Uses neutral language May use emotional language
Focuses on main ideas/key events May focus on interesting details
Does not include judgment Includes evaluation and judgment

Argument vs. Opinion

Key Differences

Sixth grade transitions from opinion writing to argument writing:

Opinion (Elementary) Argument (Middle School)
States a preference Makes a claim that can be debated
Supported by reasons Supported by reasons AND evidence
May be personal Must be based on text/evidence
Simple conclusion Addresses counterarguments

Analyzing Author's Craft

Word Choice Analysis

  • Denotation: Dictionary definition
  • Connotation: Associated feelings/ideas
  • Figurative Language: Non-literal meaning
  • Technical Language: Domain-specific terms
  • Tone: Author's attitude revealed through word choice

Structure Analysis

  • How does this sentence/paragraph/chapter fit into the whole?
  • How does structure develop theme, setting, or plot?
  • Why did the author organize the text this way?
  • How does structure affect the reader's experience?

Point of View Development

Analyzing Narrator's Point of View

Students analyze HOW the author develops the narrator's or speaker's point of view:

  • What techniques does the author use to establish POV?
  • How does the narrator's perspective shape what readers know?
  • What is revealed vs. hidden by this POV?
  • How would the story change with a different narrator?
  • Is the narrator reliable? How do we know?

📝 Lesson Ideas and Instructional Strategies

Teaching Textual Evidence

The "ICE" Method

  • Introduce: Set up the quote with context
  • Cite: Include the quote with proper citation
  • Explain: Analyze how the quote supports your point

Model Example

"In 'The Outsiders,' Ponyboy's conflicted identity is evident from the beginning. He admits, 'I lie to myself all the time. But I never believe me' (Hinton 18). This self-aware contradiction reveals his internal struggle between who he is and who society expects him to be."

Objective Summary Lesson

SWBST Summary Frame

  • Somebody (Who is the main character/subject?)
  • Wanted (What did they want?)
  • But (What was the problem/conflict?)
  • So (What did they do?)
  • Then (What was the result?)

Objectivity Practice

Have students identify and remove subjective language from sample summaries. Replace words like "amazing," "boring," "best" with neutral descriptions.

Argument Writing Lesson

Claim-Evidence-Reasoning (CER)

  1. Claim: State your position on the issue
  2. Evidence: Provide facts, quotes, data from credible sources
  3. Reasoning: Explain how the evidence supports your claim

Introducing Counterarguments

Teach students to acknowledge opposing views:

  • "Some may argue that... However..."
  • "Critics might say... Nevertheless, the evidence shows..."
  • "While it is true that... the stronger argument is..."

Author's Purpose and Point of View

SOAPSTone Analysis

  • Speaker: Who is the voice of the text?
  • Occasion: What prompted this text?
  • Audience: Who is the intended audience?
  • Purpose: Why was this written?
  • Subject: What is the text about?
  • Tone: What is the author's attitude?

Word Choice and Tone Analysis

Connotation Investigation

Examine how word choice affects meaning:

Positive Connotation Neutral Negative Connotation
slender thin scrawny
confident self-assured arrogant
thrifty economical cheap

Ask: Why might an author choose one word over another? How does it affect the reader's perception?

🔍 Close Reading and Text Analysis

Close Reading Protocol

Three-Read Strategy

  1. First Read - What does the text say? Read for gist; identify key ideas
  2. Second Read - How does the text work? Analyze craft, structure, and language
  3. Third Read - What does the text mean? Interpret deeper meaning; make connections

Genre Comparison Investigation

Essential Question

How do different genres approach similar themes and topics?

Investigation Protocol

  1. Select texts from different genres on same theme (e.g., survival)
  2. Analyze how each genre presents the theme
  3. Compare techniques: fiction uses characters; poetry uses imagery; nonfiction uses facts
  4. Evaluate which approach is most effective and why

Argument Analysis Investigation

Evaluating Claims and Evidence

Students trace and evaluate arguments in informational texts:

  • What is the author's main claim?
  • What reasons support this claim?
  • What evidence supports each reason?
  • Is the evidence sufficient and relevant?
  • Are there claims that lack support?

Media Comparison Investigation

Reading vs. Viewing/Listening

Compare experiences of text in different formats:

  1. Read a story, drama, or poem
  2. Watch or listen to an audio/video/live version
  3. Compare: What is enhanced? What is lost?
  4. Analyze how each format affects understanding

Author's Perspective Investigation

Comparing Author Presentations

Analyze how different authors present the same event or topic:

  • What facts do both authors include?
  • What does each author emphasize or de-emphasize?
  • How do word choices reveal perspective?
  • What might account for differences?

🎯 Differentiation Strategies

Supporting Struggling Readers

  • Provide texts at instructional reading level
  • Use graphic organizers and sentence frames
  • Chunk complex texts into smaller sections
  • Pre-teach vocabulary and background knowledge
  • Offer audio support for challenging texts
  • Provide evidence stems and writing frames

English Language Learners

  • Build background knowledge before reading
  • Provide visual supports and graphic organizers
  • Use bilingual resources and cognates
  • Offer sentence frames for academic discussion
  • Allow extended time for written responses
  • Partner with English-proficient peers

Gifted and Advanced Learners

  • Complex texts above grade level
  • Independent research and inquiry projects
  • Literary analysis essays with sophisticated techniques
  • Critical lens reading approaches
  • Publication opportunities
  • Mentorship and peer tutoring

❌ Common Misconceptions

Reading Misconceptions

Theme is a topic or one word

Reality: Theme is a complete statement about a topic that reveals a life lesson or universal truth.

Summary can include opinions

Reality: Objective summary includes only what the text says, without personal opinions or judgments.

Any quote counts as evidence

Reality: Evidence must directly and specifically support the claim; students must explain the connection.

Writing Misconceptions

Argument = opinion

Reality: Arguments make debatable claims supported by evidence; opinions are personal preferences.

More quotes = stronger argument

Reality: Quality over quantity; each quote needs integration and explanation.

🎓 AI Tools for Students

Guidelines for AI Use

Most AI tools require users to be 13+. Students under 13 should use AI only under teacher supervision with parental consent where required.

Appropriate Uses

Brainstorming

Generate ideas and outlines for writing, then develop independently.

Research Support

Explore topics and generate questions, then verify with credible sources.

Writing Feedback

Get suggestions for revision, then make thoughtful decisions about changes.

AI Literacy

Critical Thinking About AI

  • AI can make mistakes—always verify information
  • AI-generated text needs human revision and judgment
  • Using AI to do your work is academic dishonesty
  • AI is a tool to support learning, not replace it
