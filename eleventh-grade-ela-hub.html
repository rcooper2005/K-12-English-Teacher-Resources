📋 Eleventh Grade American Literature Standards

Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - 11th-12th Grade ELA

Reading Literary Text (ELAGSE11-12RL)

  • ELAGSE11-12RL1: Cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text, including determining where the text leaves matters uncertain
  • ELAGSE11-12RL2: Determine two or more themes or central ideas of a text and analyze their development over the course of the text, including how they interact and build on one another to produce a complex account; provide an objective summary of the text
  • ELAGSE11-12RL3: Analyze the impact of the author's choices regarding how to develop and relate elements of a story or drama
  • ELAGSE11-12RL4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in the text, including figurative and connotative meanings; analyze the impact of specific word choices on meaning and tone, including words with multiple meanings or language that is particularly fresh, engaging, or beautiful
  • ELAGSE11-12RL5: Analyze how an author's choices concerning how to structure specific parts of a text contribute to its overall structure and meaning as well as its aesthetic impact
  • ELAGSE11-12RL6: Analyze a case in which grasping point of view requires distinguishing what is directly stated in a text from what is really meant (e.g., satire, sarcasm, irony, or understatement)
  • ELAGSE11-12RL7: Analyze multiple interpretations of a story, drama, or poem, evaluating how each version interprets the source text
  • ELAGSE11-12RL9: Demonstrate knowledge of eighteenth-, nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century foundational works of American literature, including how two or more texts from the same period treat similar themes or topics
  • ELAGSE11-12RL10: By the end of grade 11, read and comprehend literature, including stories, dramas, and poems, in the grades 11-CCR text complexity band proficiently, with scaffolding as needed at the high end of the range

Reading Informational Text (ELAGSE11-12RI)

  • ELAGSE11-12RI1: Cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis, including determining where the text leaves matters uncertain
  • ELAGSE11-12RI2: Determine two or more central ideas and analyze their development, including how they interact and build on one another
  • ELAGSE11-12RI3: Analyze a complex set of ideas or sequence of events and explain how specific individuals, ideas, or events interact and develop over the course of the text
  • ELAGSE11-12RI4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases; analyze how an author uses and refines the meaning of a key term over the course of a text
  • ELAGSE11-12RI5: Analyze and evaluate the effectiveness of the structure an author uses in his or her exposition or argument
  • ELAGSE11-12RI6: Determine an author's point of view or purpose; analyze how style and content contribute to the power, persuasiveness, or beauty of the text
  • ELAGSE11-12RI7: Integrate and evaluate multiple sources of information presented in different media or formats as well as in words to address a question or solve a problem
  • ELAGSE11-12RI8: Delineate and evaluate the reasoning in seminal U.S. texts, including application of constitutional principles and use of legal reasoning and the premises, purposes, and arguments in works of public advocacy
  • ELAGSE11-12RI9: Analyze seventeenth-, eighteenth-, and nineteenth-century foundational U.S. documents of historical and literary significance for their themes, purposes, and rhetorical features
  • ELAGSE11-12RI10: By the end of grade 11, read and comprehend literary nonfiction in the grades 11-CCR text complexity band proficiently

Writing (ELAGSE11-12W)

  • ELAGSE11-12W1: Write arguments to support claims in an analysis of substantive topics or texts, using valid reasoning and relevant and sufficient evidence
  • ELAGSE11-12W2: Write informative/explanatory texts to examine and convey complex ideas, concepts, and information clearly and accurately
  • ELAGSE11-12W3: Write narratives to develop real or imagined experiences or events using effective technique, well-chosen details, and well-structured event sequences
  • ELAGSE11-12W4: Produce clear and coherent writing in which development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience
  • ELAGSE11-12W5: Develop and strengthen writing by planning, revising, editing, rewriting, or trying a new approach, focusing on addressing what is most significant for a specific purpose and audience
  • ELAGSE11-12W6: Use technology to produce, publish, and update individual or shared writing products
  • ELAGSE11-12W7: Conduct short as well as more sustained research projects to answer a question or solve a problem; narrow or broaden the inquiry when appropriate; synthesize multiple sources
  • ELAGSE11-12W8: Gather relevant information from multiple authoritative sources; assess the strengths and limitations of each source; integrate information selectively
  • ELAGSE11-12W9: Draw evidence from literary or informational texts to support analysis, reflection, and research
  • ELAGSE11-12W10: Write routinely over extended and shorter time frames for various purposes and audiences

Speaking and Listening (ELAGSE11-12SL)

  • ELAGSE11-12SL1: Initiate and participate effectively in collaborative discussions, building on others' ideas and expressing own ideas clearly and persuasively
  • ELAGSE11-12SL2: Integrate multiple sources of information presented in diverse formats and media; evaluate credibility and accuracy
  • ELAGSE11-12SL3: Evaluate a speaker's point of view, reasoning, and use of evidence and rhetoric, assessing stance, premises, links among ideas, word choice, points of emphasis, and tone
  • ELAGSE11-12SL4: Present information, findings, and supporting evidence, conveying a clear and distinct perspective
  • ELAGSE11-12SL5: Make strategic use of digital media in presentations
  • ELAGSE11-12SL6: Adapt speech to a variety of contexts and tasks, demonstrating command of formal English

Language (ELAGSE11-12L)

  • ELAGSE11-12L1: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English grammar and usage
  • ELAGSE11-12L2: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English capitalization, punctuation, and spelling
  • ELAGSE11-12L3: Apply knowledge of language to understand how language functions in different contexts; make effective choices for meaning or style; comprehend more fully when reading or listening
  • ELAGSE11-12L4: Determine or clarify the meaning of unknown and multiple-meaning words and phrases
  • ELAGSE11-12L5: Demonstrate understanding of figurative language, word relationships, and nuances in word meanings
  • ELAGSE11-12L6: Acquire and use accurately general academic and domain-specific words and phrases

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

American Literature: A Survey Approach

Eleventh grade American Literature typically surveys American literary history from the Colonial period through the contemporary era. Students analyze how American literature reflects and shapes American identity, values, and experiences while developing sophisticated analytical skills for college and career readiness.

Key Focus: Students must demonstrate knowledge of 18th-, 19th-, and early 20th-century foundational American works and analyze how texts from the same period treat similar themes or topics.

American Literary Periods

Period Dates Key Characteristics Representative Authors
Colonial/Puritan 1620-1750 Religious focus, plain style, journals, sermons Bradford, Bradstreet, Edwards
Revolutionary/Age of Reason 1750-1800 Political writing, rhetoric, Enlightenment ideals Franklin, Jefferson, Paine, Henry
Romanticism 1800-1855 Emotion, nature, individualism, imagination Irving, Cooper, Bryant, Poe
Transcendentalism 1840-1860 Self-reliance, nature, intuition, reform Emerson, Thoreau, Fuller
Realism 1855-1900 Everyday life, objective narrator, social issues Twain, James, Howells, Chopin
Naturalism 1880-1940 Determinism, survival, environment shapes fate Crane, London, Dreiser, Norris
Modernism 1900-1945 Experimentation, disillusionment, fragmentation Fitzgerald, Hemingway, Faulkner
Harlem Renaissance 1920-1940 African American identity, cultural pride Hughes, Hurston, McKay, Cullen
Postmodernism 1945-Present Questioning grand narratives, metafiction Vonnegut, Morrison, DeLillo
Contemporary 1970-Present Diverse voices, multicultural perspectives Cisneros, Alexie, Lahiri

Analyzing Satire, Irony, and Understatement

Distinguishing Direct Statement from Real Meaning

Standard RL6 requires students to analyze cases where grasping point of view requires distinguishing what is directly stated from what is really meant:

  • Satire: Using humor, irony, or ridicule to criticize (Swift, Twain)
  • Sarcasm: Mocking through opposite meaning (verbal irony with edge)
  • Irony: Contrast between appearance and reality (dramatic, situational, verbal)
  • Understatement: Deliberate minimization for effect (Hemingway)

Foundational U.S. Documents

Documents of Historical and Literary Significance

  • Colonial: Mayflower Compact, sermon literature
  • Revolutionary: Declaration of Independence, Common Sense, Federalist Papers
  • Antebellum: Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, "Ain't I a Woman?"
  • Civil War: Lincoln's speeches (Gettysburg Address, Second Inaugural)
  • Progressive Era: "The Jungle" excerpts, reform literature
  • Civil Rights: "Letter from Birmingham Jail," "I Have a Dream"

Analyzing Where Text Leaves Matters Uncertain

Ambiguity in Literary Analysis

At the 11-12 grade level, students must analyze not just what texts say but where they leave matters uncertain:

  • Ambiguous endings and interpretations
  • Unreliable narrators
  • Symbolic meanings open to interpretation
  • Tensions and contradictions within texts
  • Multiple valid interpretations

📝 Lesson Ideas

Literary Period Study

Comparing Texts from Same Period

  1. Establish historical/cultural context of period
  2. Read representative texts from period
  3. Identify common themes and techniques
  4. Analyze how different authors treat similar topics
  5. Write comparative analysis essay

Satire and Irony Analysis

What Is Really Meant

  1. Define satire, irony, sarcasm, understatement
  2. Read example texts (Twain, Swift)
  3. Identify direct statements vs. intended meaning
  4. Analyze author's true purpose and critique
  5. Discuss effectiveness of indirect communication

Foundational Document Analysis

Rhetoric in American History

  • Analyze rhetorical features (appeals, devices)
  • Evaluate argument structure and reasoning
  • Examine application of constitutional principles
  • Compare documents across periods
  • Connect to contemporary issues

Multiple Interpretations

Evaluating Different Readings

  1. Read literary text (poem, story, drama)
  2. Examine multiple critical interpretations
  3. Evaluate evidence each interpretation uses
  4. Analyze how each version interprets source
  5. Develop and defend own interpretation

🔍 Close Reading and Text Analysis

American Identity Investigation

Essential Question

How have American authors defined and questioned American identity?

Investigation Protocol

  1. Select texts from different periods addressing American identity
  2. Analyze how each author defines "American"
  3. Compare perspectives across time periods
  4. Analyze whose voices are included/excluded
  5. Synthesize findings in research essay

Ambiguity and Uncertainty Investigation

Essential Question

How do authors use ambiguity to create meaning?

Analysis Protocol

  1. Identify moments of uncertainty in text
  2. Analyze possible interpretations
  3. Evaluate evidence for each interpretation
  4. Discuss effect of ambiguity on reader
  5. Argue for interpretation while acknowledging uncertainty

Rhetorical Analysis Investigation

Essential Question

How do seminal U.S. texts use rhetoric to persuade?

Investigation Steps

  1. Select foundational document
  2. Analyze rhetorical features and appeals
  3. Evaluate reasoning and evidence
  4. Assess historical and contemporary impact
  5. Write rhetorical analysis essay

❌ Common Misconceptions

American literature starts with the colonies

Reality: Indigenous oral traditions predate European settlement.

Literary periods have clear boundaries

Reality: Periods overlap and blend; movements coexist.

Satire means the author is joking

Reality: Satire uses humor to make serious criticism.
