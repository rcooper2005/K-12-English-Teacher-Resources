📋 Third Grade ELA Standards

Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Third Grade ELA

Reading Literary Text (ELAGSE3RL)

  • ELAGSE3RL1: Ask and answer questions to demonstrate understanding of a text, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for the answers
  • ELAGSE3RL2: Recount stories, including fables, folktales, and myths from diverse cultures; determine the central message, lesson, or moral and explain how it is conveyed through key details
  • ELAGSE3RL3: Describe characters in a story (traits, motivations, feelings) and explain how their actions contribute to the sequence of events
  • ELAGSE3RL4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, distinguishing literal from nonliteral language
  • ELAGSE3RL5: Refer to parts of stories, dramas, and poems when writing or speaking about a text, using terms such as chapter, scene, and stanza
  • ELAGSE3RL6: Distinguish their own point of view from that of the narrator or those of the characters
  • ELAGSE3RL7: Explain how specific aspects of a text's illustrations contribute to what is conveyed by the words
  • ELAGSE3RL9: Compare and contrast themes, settings, and plots of stories written by the same author about the same or similar characters
  • ELAGSE3RL10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literature at the high end of the grades 2-3 text complexity band independently and proficiently

Reading Informational Text (ELAGSE3RI)

  • ELAGSE3RI1: Ask and answer questions to demonstrate understanding of a text, referring explicitly to the text as the basis for the answers
  • ELAGSE3RI2: Determine the main idea of a text; recount the key details and explain how they support the main idea
  • ELAGSE3RI3: Describe the relationship between a series of historical events, scientific ideas or concepts, or steps in technical procedures, using language that pertains to time, sequence, and cause/effect
  • ELAGSE3RI4: Determine the meaning of general academic and domain-specific words and phrases in a text relevant to a grade 3 topic or subject area
  • ELAGSE3RI5: Use text features and search tools to locate information relevant to a given topic efficiently
  • ELAGSE3RI6: Distinguish their own point of view from that of the author of a text
  • ELAGSE3RI7: Use information gained from illustrations and the words in a text to demonstrate understanding of the text
  • ELAGSE3RI8: Describe the logical connection between particular sentences and paragraphs in a text (comparison, cause/effect, first/second/third in a sequence)
  • ELAGSE3RI9: Compare and contrast the most important points and key details presented in two texts on the same topic
  • ELAGSE3RI10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend informational texts at the high end of the grades 2-3 text complexity band independently and proficiently

Reading Foundational Skills (ELAGSE3RF)

  • ELAGSE3RF3: Know and apply grade-level phonics and word analysis skills in decoding words
  • ELAGSE3RF3a: Identify and know the meaning of the most common prefixes and derivational suffixes
  • ELAGSE3RF3b: Decode words with common Latin suffixes
  • ELAGSE3RF3c: Decode multisyllable words
  • ELAGSE3RF3d: Read grade-appropriate irregularly spelled words
  • ELAGSE3RF4: Read with sufficient accuracy and fluency to support comprehension
  • ELAGSE3RF4a: Read grade-level text with purpose and understanding
  • ELAGSE3RF4b: Read grade-level prose and poetry orally with accuracy, appropriate rate, and expression
  • ELAGSE3RF4c: Use context to confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, rereading as necessary

Writing (ELAGSE3W)

  • ELAGSE3W1: Write opinion pieces on topics or texts, supporting a point of view with reasons
  • ELAGSE3W2: Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas and information clearly
  • ELAGSE3W3: Write narratives to develop real or imagined experiences or events using effective technique, descriptive details, and clear event sequences
  • ELAGSE3W4: With guidance and support from adults, produce writing in which the development and organization are appropriate to task and purpose
  • ELAGSE3W5: With guidance and support from peers and adults, develop and strengthen writing by planning, revising, and editing
  • ELAGSE3W6: With guidance and support from adults, use technology to produce and publish writing
  • ELAGSE3W7: Conduct short research projects that build knowledge about a topic
  • ELAGSE3W8: Recall information from experiences or gather information from print and digital sources
  • ELAGSE3W10: Write routinely over extended time frames and shorter time frames for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences

Speaking and Listening (ELAGSE3SL)

  • ELAGSE3SL1: Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions with diverse partners on grade 3 topics and texts
  • ELAGSE3SL2: Determine the main ideas and supporting details of a text read aloud or information presented in diverse media and formats
  • ELAGSE3SL3: Ask and answer questions about information from a speaker, offering appropriate elaboration and detail
  • ELAGSE3SL4: Report on a topic or text, tell a story, or recount an experience with appropriate facts and relevant, descriptive details
  • ELAGSE3SL5: Create engaging audio recordings of stories or poems that demonstrate fluid reading at an understandable pace
  • ELAGSE3SL6: Speak in complete sentences when appropriate to task and situation

Language (ELAGSE3L)

  • ELAGSE3L1: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English grammar and usage when writing or speaking
  • ELAGSE3L2: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English capitalization, punctuation, and spelling when writing
  • ELAGSE3L3: Use knowledge of language and its conventions when writing, speaking, reading, or listening
  • ELAGSE3L4: Determine or clarify the meaning of unknown and multiple-meaning words and phrases based on grade 3 reading and content
  • ELAGSE3L5: Demonstrate understanding of word relationships and nuances in word meanings
  • ELAGSE3L6: Acquire and use accurately grade-appropriate conversational, general academic, and domain-specific words and phrases

Science of Reading Standards - Third Grade

Advanced Phonics and Word Analysis

  • Decode multisyllable words using knowledge of syllable types (closed, open, VCe, vowel teams, r-controlled, consonant-le)

  • Recognize and read grade-level irregular/sight words automatically
  • Use syllabication patterns to decode unfamiliar multisyllable words
  • Recognize common Latin roots (port, form, ject, spect, dict)

Fluency Development

  • Beginning of year: 70-90 WCPM with accuracy of 95%+
  • End of year: 100-120 WCPM with accuracy of 97%+
  • Read with appropriate prosody (expression, phrasing, intonation)
  • Adjust reading rate based on purpose and text difficulty
  • Self-monitor and self-correct during reading

Vocabulary Development

  • Use context clues to determine meaning of unknown words
  • Use knowledge of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots) to determine meaning
  • Distinguish between literal and figurative language
  • Understand and use academic vocabulary across content areas
  • Recognize and understand multiple-meaning words

Comprehension Strategies

  • Determine main idea and supporting details
  • Make inferences using text evidence
  • Summarize texts accurately and concisely
  • Compare and contrast texts, themes, and author's purpose
  • Use text features to locate and comprehend information
  • Monitor comprehension and apply fix-up strategies

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

The Third Grade Reading Transition

Third grade marks a critical shift from "learning to read" to "reading to learn." Students transition from developing foundational skills to applying those skills to gain knowledge across all content areas. Research identifies the "third grade reading guarantee" as a crucial milestone—students who aren't reading proficiently by the end of third grade are four times more likely to not graduate from high school.

Key Transition: Third grade is when the focus shifts from decoding to comprehension. Students should be automatic decoders by now, freeing cognitive resources for understanding complex texts and building content knowledge.

Scarborough's Reading Rope - Third Grade Focus

Word Recognition Strands (Increasingly Automatic)

  • Phonological Awareness: Should be nearly complete; focus on manipulation of sounds in multisyllable words
  • Decoding: Apply advanced phonics patterns, morphology, and syllabication
  • Sight Recognition: Automatic recognition of grade-level and below words

Language Comprehension Strands (Increasingly Strategic)

  • Background Knowledge: Actively build content knowledge through reading
  • Vocabulary: Learn academic and domain-specific vocabulary; use morphology
  • Language Structures: Understand complex sentences and text structures
  • Verbal Reasoning: Make inferences, draw conclusions, think critically
  • Literacy Knowledge: Apply genre knowledge and comprehension strategies

Morphological Awareness

Third grade introduces systematic instruction in morphology—the study of word parts and their meanings. This significantly impacts vocabulary development and reading comprehension.

Morpheme Type Examples Instructional Focus
Prefixes un-, re-, pre-, dis-, mis-, non- How prefixes change word meaning
Suffixes -ful, -less, -ly, -er, -est, -ment, -ness How suffixes change part of speech
Latin Roots port (carry), form (shape), spect (see) Using roots to unlock word meaning
Compound Words anything, everyone, understand Breaking compounds into meaningful parts

Text Complexity and the Staircase

Third graders engage with increasingly complex texts across the 2-3 grade band. The three dimensions of text complexity include:

  • Quantitative: Lexile range 420L-820L (grades 2-3 band)
  • Qualitative: Text structure, language features, knowledge demands, meaning/purpose
  • Reader and Task: Student motivation, prior knowledge, task purpose

Fluency Development

Fluency Components

  • Accuracy: Reading words correctly (target: 97%+ for instructional level)
  • Rate: Words correct per minute (WCPM) - end of year goal: 100-120 WCPM
  • Prosody: Expression, phrasing, and attention to punctuation

NAEP Oral Reading Fluency Scale

  • Level 4: Reads primarily in larger, meaningful phrase groups with expressive interpretation
  • Level 3: Reads primarily in three- or four-word phrase groups with some expression
  • Level 2: Reads primarily in two-word phrases with some three- or four-word groupings
  • Level 1: Reads word-by-word with occasional two-word or three-word phrases

Comprehension Strategy Instruction

Third graders need explicit instruction in comprehension strategies with gradual release to independent application:

  • Making Connections: Text-to-self, text-to-text, text-to-world
  • Questioning: Generating and answering questions before, during, and after reading
  • Visualizing: Creating mental images to support comprehension
  • Inferring: Reading between the lines using text evidence and background knowledge
  • Determining Importance: Identifying main ideas and key details
  • Synthesizing: Combining information to create new understanding
  • Monitoring: Checking for understanding and applying fix-up strategies

Writing Development

Third graders expand their writing across three text types with increasing sophistication:

Opinion Writing

  • Clear opinion statement
  • Organized reasons with supporting details
  • Linking words (because, therefore, since)
  • Concluding statement

Informative Writing

  • Topic introduction
  • Facts, definitions, details grouped by subtopic
  • Linking words (also, another, and, more)
  • Concluding statement

Narrative Writing

  • Establish situation and narrator/characters
  • Dialogue and descriptions of actions, thoughts, feelings
  • Temporal words (first, next, then, finally)
  • Sense of closure

📝 Lesson Ideas and Instructional Strategies

Morphology Lesson: Prefixes that Mean "Not"

Objective

Students will identify and use prefixes un-, dis-, non-, and mis- to determine word meanings.

Materials

  • Prefix anchor chart
  • Word cards with base words
  • Interactive notebooks

Procedure

  1. Introduction (5 min): Show words "happy" and "unhappy" - discuss how the meaning changed. Explain that prefixes are word parts added to the beginning that change meaning.
  2. Explicit Teaching (10 min): Introduce each prefix with examples: un- (unfair), dis- (disagree), non- (nonfiction), mis- (misunderstand). All mean "not" or "opposite of."
  3. Guided Practice (10 min): Give base words; students add prefixes and explain new meanings: kind→unkind, obey→disobey, sense→nonsense, behave→misbehave
  4. Independent Practice (10 min): Word sort activity—students sort words by prefix and write sentences using three words.
  5. Closure (5 min): Exit ticket: Add a prefix to make the opposite: honest, trust, stop, agree

Close Reading Lesson: Finding Main Idea and Details

Objective

Students will determine the main idea of an informational text and identify key supporting details.

Text Selection

Choose a grade-level informational text (Lexile 600-800) with clear topic sentences and supporting details.

Close Reading Protocol (3 Reads)

  1. First Read - What does the text say? Read for gist. Students identify the topic. "This text is mostly about..."
  2. Second Read - How does the text work? Annotate topic sentences. Underline key details that support the main idea. Use symbols: ★ main idea, → supporting detail
  3. Third Read - What does the text mean? Summarize using main idea + key details. Discuss author's purpose and text structure.

Graphic Organizer

Use a "Main Idea Table" - main idea is the tabletop, supporting details are the legs that hold it up.

Writing Workshop: Opinion Writing with Reasons

Mini-Lesson: Organizing Reasons

Teach students to use OREO structure: Opinion, Reasons, Examples, Opinion restated

Procedure

  1. Connection: "Writers, we've been working on stating our opinions clearly. Today we'll learn how to organize our reasons to convince our readers."
  2. Teaching: Model using mentor text. Show how the author states opinion, gives reasons with examples, and uses linking words (because, for example, also).
  3. Active Engagement: Give students a simple opinion topic (e.g., "Recess should be longer"). Partners brainstorm 3 reasons with examples.
  4. Link: "Today during writing time, organize your reasons from strongest to weakest. Use linking words to connect your ideas."
  5. Independent Writing: 20-30 minutes of sustained writing with teacher conferring
  6. Share: Partners read opening paragraph and one reason. Listeners give feedback: "I was convinced because..."
Linking Words Anchor Chart: To give reasons: because, since, for example, such as. To add reasons: also, another, in addition, furthermore. To conclude: therefore, for these reasons, in conclusion.

Fluency Lesson: Reader's Theater

Objective

Students will read with appropriate expression, phrasing, and rate through repeated readings of a script.

Weekly Schedule

Day Activity
Monday Teacher reads script aloud modeling fluency. Discuss story elements and character traits.
Tuesday Assign parts. Echo reading - teacher reads a line, students repeat with same expression.
Wednesday Choral reading in groups. Practice highlighting punctuation cues for expression.
Thursday Small group rehearsal with peer feedback on expression and rate.
Friday Performance for class or partner class. Self-reflection on fluency growth.

Vocabulary Lesson: Context Clues

Teaching the PAVE Strategy

Predict the meaning, Analyze context clues, Verify with dictionary, Evaluate your prediction

Types of Context Clues

  • Definition: The author directly tells you the meaning
  • Synonym: A word with similar meaning is nearby
  • Antonym: A word with opposite meaning is nearby
  • Example: Examples help explain the word
  • Inference: You figure it out from surrounding sentences

Practice Sentences

"The frigid water was so cold that my toes went numb." (Definition clue)

"Unlike his timid sister, Jake was bold and fearless." (Antonym clue)

"Nutritious foods like vegetables, fruits, and whole grains keep you healthy." (Example clue)

🔍 Close Reading and Text Analysis

Close Reading Investigation: Comparing Folktales

Essential Question

How do different cultures tell similar stories, and what can we learn from the differences?

Text Set: Cinderella Stories

  • "Cinderella" (Perrault, European)
  • "Yeh-Shen" (Chinese)
  • "The Rough-Face Girl" (Algonquin Native American)
  • "Adelita" (Mexican)

Investigation Protocol

  1. Day 1: Read first version closely. Chart story elements: setting, main character, helper, transformation, ending.
  2. Day 2-3: Read additional versions. Add to comparison chart. Note similarities and differences.
  3. Day 4: Analyze: What themes appear in all versions? What cultural details are unique? Why might cultures have similar stories?
  4. Day 5: Synthesis: Write a compare/contrast paragraph or create your own cultural version.

Text Structure Investigation

Learning Target

Identify and analyze how authors use text structures to organize information.

Text Structure Types

  • Description: Describes a topic with details and characteristics
  • Sequence/Order: Events or steps in order (signal words: first, next, then, finally)
  • Compare/Contrast: Shows how things are alike and different (signal words: both, however, unlike)
  • Cause/Effect: Shows why something happened and what resulted (signal words: because, so, therefore, as a result)
  • Problem/Solution: Presents a problem and how it was solved

Investigation Activity

  1. Read short passages representing each text structure
  2. Highlight signal words in each passage
  3. Match passages to graphic organizers (Venn diagram, flow chart, cause-effect chart, etc.)
  4. Explain how the structure helps readers understand the information

Author's Purpose Investigation

PIE Framework

  • Persuade - convince the reader to think or do something
  • Inform - teach the reader about a topic
  • Entertain - amuse or engage the reader with a story

Text Evidence Hunt

Give students various texts (advertisement, encyclopedia article, fairy tale, recipe, editorial, poem). Students determine purpose and cite specific evidence:

  • "The author's purpose is to _____ because the text _____."
  • "I know this is meant to persuade/inform/entertain because _____."

Character Analysis Investigation

STEAL Method for Character Analysis

Authors reveal character through:

  • Speech - What does the character say?
  • Thoughts - What is the character thinking?
  • Effect on Others - How do other characters react?
  • Actions - What does the character do?
  • Looks - How is the character described?

Character Traits Investigation

  1. Choose a main character from current read-aloud or novel
  2. Complete STEAL chart with text evidence
  3. Infer 3 character traits based on evidence
  4. Write a paragraph: "In [title], [character] is [trait] because..."

Research Investigation: Animal Adaptations

Inquiry Question

How do animals adapt to survive in their environments?

Research Process

  1. Wonder: Generate questions about an animal of choice
  2. Investigate: Use books and approved websites to gather information
  3. Synthesize: Organize information into categories (habitat, diet, physical adaptations, behavioral adaptations)
  4. Express: Create an informative poster, report, or digital presentation
  5. Reflect: What did you learn? What new questions do you have?

Note-Taking Template

Teach students to paraphrase rather than copy. Model: "The book says... In my own words..."

Use fact cards or graphic organizers to collect information from multiple sources.

📊 Assessment Strategies

Formative Assessment Tools

Exit Tickets

Quick checks for understanding at the end of lessons:

  • 3-2-1: 3 things learned, 2 questions, 1 connection
  • Ticket Out the Door: Answer one question on a sticky note
  • Muddy/Clear: What's still confusing? What's clear?

Quick Writes

Timed writing to assess comprehension or skill application:

  • Summarize the main idea in 3 sentences
  • Write about the character's feelings and why
  • Explain how you figured out an unknown word

Thumbs Up/Down/Sideways

Quick whole-class check for understanding. Students show thumbs to indicate their confidence level.

Reading Assessments

Running Records

Ongoing assessment of oral reading to determine accuracy, fluency, and reading level. Calculate:

  • Accuracy Rate: (Words correct ÷ Total words) × 100
  • Independent Level: 95-100% accuracy
  • Instructional Level: 90-94% accuracy
  • Frustration Level: Below 90% accuracy

DIBELS/Acadience Reading

Universal screening measures for third grade:

  • ORF (Oral Reading Fluency): Words correct per minute from grade-level passages
  • Retell: Comprehension check after reading
  • Maze: Silent reading comprehension measure
Benchmark Beginning of Year Middle of Year End of Year
ORF (WCPM) 70 92 110

Writing Assessments

Writing Rubrics by Genre

Use rubrics aligned to standards for each writing type:

  • Opinion: Clear opinion, organized reasons, linking words, conclusion
  • Informative: Topic introduction, facts and details, linking words, conclusion
  • Narrative: Situation/characters, sequence of events, dialogue/description, closure

Include criteria for conventions: grammar, punctuation, spelling

Writing Conferences

One-on-one assessment and teaching during writing workshop:

  1. Research: "What are you working on as a writer?"
  2. Compliment: Note something the student is doing well
  3. Teach: Offer one teaching point based on need
  4. Link: Remind student to use this strategy going forward

Comprehension Assessments

Reading Response Questions

Assess comprehension with text-dependent questions:

  • Key Ideas: What is the main idea? What key details support it?
  • Craft and Structure: What does the word ___ mean in this context? How does the author organize this text?
  • Integration: How do the illustrations help you understand ___? Compare this text to ___.

Require students to cite text evidence: "According to the text..." or "On page ___, it says..."

Student Self-Assessment

Reading Logs with Reflection

Students track reading and reflect on their growth:

  • What reading goal are you working on?
  • What strategy did you use today when you got stuck?
  • How did your reading go today? What will you work on tomorrow?

Vocabulary Instruction Strategies

Robust Vocabulary Instruction (Beck, McKeown, Kucan)

  1. Introduce word in context from text
  2. Provide student-friendly definition
  3. Give examples in various contexts
  4. Have students interact with the word
  5. Provide multiple exposures over time

❌ Common Misconceptions

Reading Misconceptions

Misconception: Fast reading = good reading

Reality: Fluency includes rate, accuracy, AND prosody. Reading too fast can hurt comprehension. Good readers adjust their rate based on text difficulty and purpose.

Address It: Model adjusting reading rate for different texts. Teach prosody explicitly. Emphasize that understanding matters more than speed.

Misconception: Main idea is the first sentence

Reality: Main idea can be stated anywhere in a paragraph or may be implied. Students must consider all information to determine what the text is mostly about.

Address It: Practice with paragraphs where main idea is in different positions. Teach the difference between topic and main idea.

Misconception: Fiction = fake, Nonfiction = true

Reality: Fiction is imaginative but can contain true elements. Nonfiction aims to inform but may include bias or errors. Critical reading applies to both.

Address It: Discuss historical fiction, realistic fiction, and how to evaluate nonfiction sources.

Misconception: All characters are good or bad

Reality: Characters are complex with multiple traits. They can change throughout a story. Even protagonists have flaws.

Address It: Analyze characters using STEAL method. Discuss character change and growth. Use character trait continuums.

Writing Misconceptions

Misconception: More is always better

Reality: Quality matters more than quantity. Strong writing is focused, clear, and uses precise words. Adding unnecessary details weakens writing.

Address It: Teach revision for focus. Practice "tightening" writing by removing unnecessary words. Compare short, powerful sentences to wordy ones.

Misconception: Revising = editing

Reality: Revising focuses on meaning, organization, and development. Editing focuses on conventions. Both are important but different processes.

Address It: Separate revision and editing in the writing process. Use different checklists for each. Model both processes explicitly.

Misconception: Good writers get it right the first time

Reality: All writers revise. The writing process involves multiple drafts. Published authors do extensive revision.

Address It: Share author interviews about revision. Show drafts of mentor texts. Celebrate growth through revision.

Grammar and Spelling Misconceptions

Misconception: "Sound it out" works for all words

Reality: English has many irregular spellings that don't follow patterns. Some words must be memorized (sight words).

Address It: Teach regular patterns AND irregular words. Use word walls and practice with high-frequency words. Discuss word origins.

Misconception: Adding -ed always makes past tense

Reality: English has many irregular past tense verbs (go→went, run→ran, eat→ate). The -ed ending also has different sounds (/t/, /d/, /id/).

Address It: Explicitly teach irregular verbs. Sort verbs by regular/irregular. Practice with common irregular verbs.

Misconception: Sentences can't start with "And" or "But"

Reality: Professional writers often start sentences with coordinating conjunctions for effect. It's a stylistic choice, not a rule.

Address It: Find examples in mentor texts. Discuss when it's effective versus overused.

Vocabulary Misconceptions

Misconception: Looking up every word in the dictionary

Reality: Good readers use multiple strategies including context clues, word parts, and sometimes skipping words that aren't essential.

Address It: Teach context clues strategies. Practice morphology. Help students identify when they need a dictionary versus when they can figure it out.

Windows, Mirrors, and Sliding Glass Doors

Dr. Rudine Sims Bishop's framework reminds us that children need:

  • Mirrors: Books that reflect their own lives, cultures, and experiences
  • Windows: Books that offer views into others' experiences and cultures
  • Sliding Glass Doors: Books that allow readers to walk through and become part of another world

  • Audit classroom library for diverse representation

Teaching AI Literacy

Conversations About AI

  • What is AI? A computer program that can learn and help with tasks
  • AI makes mistakes: Always check information from AI
  • AI can't replace thinking: You still need to learn, think, and do your own work
  • AI learns from people: The information AI has came from humans
  • Being safe online: Never share personal information with AI
Introducing AI Responsibly: Start conversations about AI as a tool that helps us—like a calculator for math or a dictionary for words. Emphasize that our own thinking, creativity, and effort are what make learning valuable.
