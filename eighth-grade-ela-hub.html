📋 Eighth Grade ELA Standards

Georgia Standards of Excellence (GSE) - Eighth Grade ELA

Reading Literary Text (ELAGSE8RL)

  • ELAGSE8RL1: Cite the textual evidence that most strongly supports an analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text
  • ELAGSE8RL2: Determine a theme or central idea of a text and analyze its development over the course of the text, including its relationship to the characters, setting, and plot; provide an objective summary of the text
  • ELAGSE8RL3: Analyze how particular lines of dialogue or incidents in a story or drama propel the action, reveal aspects of a character, or provoke a decision
  • ELAGSE8RL4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, including figurative and connotative meanings; analyze the impact of specific word choices on meaning and tone, including analogies or allusions to other texts
  • ELAGSE8RL5: Compare and contrast the structure of two or more texts and analyze how the differing structure of each text contributes to its meaning and style
  • ELAGSE8RL6: Analyze how differences in the points of view of the characters and the audience or reader create such effects as suspense or humor
  • ELAGSE8RL7: Analyze the extent to which a filmed or live production of a story or drama stays faithful to or departs from the text or script, evaluating the choices made by the director or actors
  • ELAGSE8RL9: Analyze how a modern work of fiction draws on themes, patterns of events, or character types from myths, traditional stories, or religious works, including describing how the material is rendered new
  • ELAGSE8RL10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literature, including stories, dramas, and poems, at the high end of grades 6-8 text complexity band independently and proficiently

Reading Informational Text (ELAGSE8RI)

  • ELAGSE8RI1: Cite the textual evidence that most strongly supports an analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text
  • ELAGSE8RI2: Determine a central idea of a text and analyze its development over the course of the text, including its relationship to supporting ideas; provide an objective summary of the text
  • ELAGSE8RI3: Analyze how a text makes connections among and distinctions between individuals, ideas, or events
  • ELAGSE8RI4: Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in a text, including figurative, connotative, and technical meanings; analyze the impact of specific word choices on meaning and tone, including analogies or allusions to other texts
  • ELAGSE8RI5: Analyze in detail the structure of a specific paragraph in a text, including the role of particular sentences in developing and refining a key concept
  • ELAGSE8RI6: Determine an author's point of view or purpose in a text and analyze how the author acknowledges and responds to conflicting evidence or viewpoints
  • ELAGSE8RI7: Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of using different mediums to present a particular topic or idea
  • ELAGSE8RI8: Delineate and evaluate the argument and specific claims in a text, assessing whether the reasoning is sound and the evidence is relevant and sufficient; recognize when irrelevant evidence is introduced
  • ELAGSE8RI9: Analyze a case in which two or more texts provide conflicting information on the same topic and identify where the texts disagree on matters of fact or interpretation
  • ELAGSE8RI10: By the end of the year, read and comprehend literary nonfiction at the high end of the grades 6-8 text complexity band independently and proficiently

Writing (ELAGSE8W)

  • ELAGSE8W1: Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence
    • a. Introduce claim(s), acknowledge and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically
    • b. Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant evidence, using accurate, credible sources and demonstrating an understanding of the topic or text
    • c. Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence
    • d. Establish and maintain a formal style
    • e. Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented
  • ELAGSE8W2: Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas, concepts, and information through the selection, organization, and analysis of relevant content
  • ELAGSE8W3: Write narratives to develop real or imagined experiences or events using effective technique, relevant descriptive details, and well-structured event sequences
  • ELAGSE8W4: Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience
  • ELAGSE8W5: With some guidance and support from peers and adults, develop and strengthen writing as needed by planning, revising, editing, rewriting, or trying a new approach, focusing on how well purpose and audience have been addressed
  • ELAGSE8W6: Use technology, including the Internet, to produce and publish writing and present the relationships between information and ideas efficiently as well as to interact and collaborate with others
  • ELAGSE8W7: Conduct short research projects to answer a question, drawing on several sources and generating additional related, focused questions that allow for multiple avenues of exploration
  • ELAGSE8W8: Gather relevant information from multiple print and digital sources, using search terms effectively; assess the credibility and accuracy of each source; and quote or paraphrase the data and conclusions of others while avoiding plagiarism and following a standard format for citation
  • ELAGSE8W9: Draw evidence from literary or informational texts to support analysis, reflection, and research
  • ELAGSE8W10: Write routinely over extended time frames and shorter time frames for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences

Speaking and Listening (ELAGSE8SL)

  • ELAGSE8SL1: Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions with diverse partners on grade 8 topics, texts, and issues, building on others' ideas and expressing their own clearly
  • ELAGSE8SL2: Analyze the purpose of information presented in diverse media and formats and evaluate the motives behind its presentation
  • ELAGSE8SL3: Delineate a speaker's argument and specific claims, evaluating the soundness of the reasoning and relevance and sufficiency of the evidence and identifying when irrelevant evidence is introduced
  • ELAGSE8SL4: Present claims and findings, emphasizing salient points in a focused, coherent manner with relevant evidence, sound valid reasoning, and well-chosen details; use appropriate eye contact, adequate volume, and clear pronunciation
  • ELAGSE8SL5: Integrate multimedia and visual displays into presentations to clarify information, strengthen claims and evidence, and add interest
  • ELAGSE8SL6: Adapt speech to a variety of contexts and tasks, demonstrating command of formal English when indicated or appropriate

Language (ELAGSE8L)

  • ELAGSE8L1: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English grammar and usage when writing or speaking
    • a. Explain the function of verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives) in general and their function in particular sentences
    • b. Form and use verbs in the active and passive voice
    • c. Form and use verbs in the indicative, imperative, interrogative, conditional, and subjunctive mood
    • d. Recognize and correct inappropriate shifts in verb voice and mood
  • ELAGSE8L2: Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English capitalization, punctuation, and spelling when writing
  • ELAGSE8L3: Use knowledge of language and its conventions when writing, speaking, reading, or listening
  • ELAGSE8L4: Determine or clarify the meaning of unknown and multiple-meaning words or phrases based on grade 8 reading and content
  • ELAGSE8L5: Demonstrate understanding of figurative language, word relationships, and nuances in word meanings
  • ELAGSE8L6: Acquire and use accurately grade-appropriate general academic and domain-specific words and phrases

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Eighth Grade as Capstone of Middle School

Eighth grade represents the culmination of middle school literacy and preparation for high school expectations. Students move from analyzing to evaluating, from citing evidence to citing the MOST STRONGLY supporting evidence, and from identifying to recognizing irrelevant evidence. This is the year to solidify analytical skills before the increased rigor of high school.

Key Progression: 7th grade = cite several pieces of evidence, analyze interactions; 8th grade = cite MOST STRONGLY supporting evidence, EVALUATE soundness, recognize IRRELEVANT evidence

Selecting the Strongest Evidence

Teaching Evidence Evaluation

Students must learn to evaluate evidence quality, not just find evidence:

  • Directness: Does it directly address the claim?
  • Specificity: Is it specific rather than general?
  • Credibility: Is the source reliable?
  • Sufficiency: Is there enough to prove the point?

Teaching Strategy

Present multiple pieces of evidence and have students rank them from strongest to weakest, justifying their ranking.

Analyzing Dialogue and Incidents

How Dialogue Functions

Eighth graders analyze how specific dialogue and incidents:

  • Propel action: Move the plot forward
  • Reveal character: Show personality, motivation, values
  • Provoke decision: Lead to character choices

Analysis Questions

  • What does this dialogue reveal about the character?
  • How does this incident change the direction of the story?
  • What decision does this dialogue/incident provoke?
  • How would the story be different without this moment?

Point of View and Effect

POV Creates Suspense and Humor

Eighth grade analyzes HOW differences in POV create specific effects:

  • Dramatic Irony: Audience knows more than character (creates suspense)
  • Character vs. Reader Knowledge: Discrepancy creates humor or tension
  • Multiple Perspectives: Different characters understand events differently

Example Analysis

In Romeo and Juliet, the audience knows Juliet isn't really dead (dramatic irony), which creates intense suspense as Romeo acts on incomplete knowledge.

Intertextual Analysis

Modern Works and Traditional Sources

Eighth graders analyze how modern fiction draws on and transforms:

  • Greek and Roman mythology
  • Norse mythology
  • Biblical narratives and allusions
  • Traditional fairy tales and folklore
  • Classic literature

Analysis Focus

Not just "this is like that myth" but HOW the material is "rendered new"—what changes, what stays the same, and WHY.

Grammar: Verbals and Verb Moods

Verbal Type Definition Example
Gerund Verb form (-ing) used as noun Swimming is good exercise.
Participle Verb form used as adjective The running water was cold.
Infinitive To + verb, used as noun/adj/adv I want to swim.
Verb Mood Use Example
Indicative States facts or asks questions She writes every day.
Imperative Gives commands Write your essay now.
Interrogative Asks questions Does she write daily?
Conditional Expresses conditions She would write if she had time.
Subjunctive Expresses wishes, demands, suggestions I wish she were here.

📝 Lesson Ideas

Selecting Strongest Evidence

Evidence Ranking Activity

  1. Present a claim about a text
  2. Provide 4-5 pieces of potential evidence
  3. Students rank from strongest to weakest
  4. Discuss criteria for "strongest" (direct, specific, credible)
  5. Write response using only the strongest evidence

Dialogue and Incident Analysis

Three-Column Analysis

Dialogue/Incident Function Effect on Story
Quote or describe scene Propels action / Reveals character / Provokes decision How it changes the story

Dramatic Irony Lesson

Creating Suspense Through POV

  1. Define dramatic irony (audience knows more than character)
  2. Read scene with dramatic irony
  3. Identify: What does audience know? What doesn't character know?
  4. Analyze: How does this create suspense?
  5. Write: Create original scene using dramatic irony

Modern Adaptations of Classic Sources

Tracing Transformations

  1. Read original myth or traditional story
  2. Read modern adaptation (Percy Jackson, Circe, etc.)
  3. Compare: What stays the same? What changes?
  4. Analyze: How is the material "rendered new"?
  5. Evaluate: Why might author have made these changes?

Evaluating Filmed Productions

Text to Film Analysis

  1. Read the original text
  2. View the filmed/staged production
  3. Identify faithful adaptations vs. departures
  4. Analyze director's/actor's choices
  5. Evaluate: Were changes effective? Why/why not?

🔍 Close Reading and Text Analysis

Conflicting Information Investigation

Essential Question

How do we analyze texts that provide conflicting information on the same topic?

Investigation Protocol

  1. Select texts with conflicting accounts of same event/topic
  2. Read and annotate each text
  3. Identify points of agreement
  4. Identify points of disagreement (fact vs. interpretation)
  5. Analyze why texts might conflict
  6. Evaluate which account is more credible and why

Intertextual Analysis Investigation

Essential Question

How do modern works of fiction transform traditional sources?

Investigation Protocol

  1. Study original source (myth, religious text, traditional story)
  2. Read modern adaptation
  3. Map connections: themes, character types, patterns of events
  4. Analyze transformations: What is "rendered new"?
  5. Evaluate purpose: Why transform this source?

Argument Evaluation Investigation

Essential Question

How do we evaluate the soundness of reasoning and sufficiency of evidence?

Evaluation Criteria

  • Is the reasoning sound (logical, free of fallacies)?
  • Is the evidence relevant (directly supports claims)?
  • Is the evidence sufficient (enough to prove the point)?
  • Is there irrelevant evidence introduced?

Structure Comparison Investigation

Essential Question

How does the differing structure of texts contribute to meaning and style?

Analysis Questions

  • What structure does each text use?
  • How does structure contribute to meaning?
  • How does structure contribute to style?
  • Why might authors choose different structures for similar content?

Argument Writing Rubric Elements

❌ Common Misconceptions

All evidence is equally strong

Reality: Some evidence supports claims more directly and specifically than others.

More evidence is always better

Reality: Quality matters more than quantity; irrelevant evidence weakens arguments.

Adaptations should be faithful to originals

Reality: Effective adaptations "render material new" while honoring core elements.
