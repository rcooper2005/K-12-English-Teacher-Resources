  • ELAGSEKSL5: Add drawings or other visual displays to descriptions as desired
  • ELAGSEKSL6: Speak audibly and express thoughts, feelings, and ideas clearly

Language (ELAGSEKL)

  • ELAGSEKL1: Demonstrate command of conventions of standard English grammar and usage
  • ELAGSEKL2: Demonstrate command of conventions of standard English capitalization, punctuation, spelling
  • ELAGSEKL4: Determine or clarify the meaning of unknown and multiple-meaning words and phrases
  • ELAGSEKL5: With guidance and support, explore word relationships and nuances in word meanings
  • ELAGSEKL6: Use words and phrases acquired through conversations, reading, and being read to

Science of Reading Standards

Phonological Awareness

  • Recognize and produce rhyming words
  • Count, pronounce, blend, and segment syllables in spoken words
  • Blend and segment onsets and rimes of single-syllable words
  • Isolate and pronounce initial, medial vowel, and final sounds in CVC words
  • Add or substitute individual sounds in simple, one-syllable words

Phonics and Word Recognition

  • Demonstrate one-to-one letter-sound correspondences for each consonant
  • Associate long and short sounds with common spellings for the five major vowels
  • Read common high-frequency words by sight
  • Distinguish between similarly spelled words by identifying different sounds

Fluency

  • Read emergent-reader texts with purpose and understanding
  • Develop automaticity in letter and sound recognition
  • Practice reading with appropriate rate, accuracy, and expression

Vocabulary Development

  • Develop rich oral vocabulary through conversation and read-alouds
  • Learn words through explicit instruction and context
  • Connect new vocabulary to prior knowledge and experiences

Comprehension

  • Build background knowledge through diverse text exposure
  • Practice active listening strategies during read-alouds
  • Develop understanding of text structure and organization
  • Make predictions and connections to personal experiences

📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Understanding Kindergarten Literacy Development

Kindergarten is a foundational year for literacy development. Children enter with varying levels of print awareness, letter knowledge, and phonological skills. Understanding where each child is on the literacy continuum is essential for providing appropriate instruction.

The Science of Reading: Key Components

Scarborough's Reading Rope

Reading comprehension results from the interweaving of two strands:

  • Word Recognition: Phonological awareness, decoding, sight recognition
  • Language Comprehension: Background knowledge, vocabulary, language structures, verbal reasoning, literacy knowledge

Phonological Awareness Continuum

Progression of Skills:

  1. Word awareness (sentences have separate words)
  2. Syllable awareness (words have parts)
  3. Onset-rime awareness (beginning sounds and word families)
  4. Phoneme awareness (individual sounds)

Print Concepts Kindergarteners Should Master

  • Books have a front cover, back cover, and spine
  • Print carries meaning (not pictures alone)
  • We read from left to right, top to bottom
  • Words are separated by spaces
  • Letters make up words
  • There is a difference between letters and words
  • Punctuation marks serve a purpose

Emergent Writing Stages

  1. Drawing: Pictures represent ideas
  2. Scribbling: Mock writing appears
  3. Letter-like forms: Symbols resemble letters
  4. Random letters: Real letters without sound connection
  5. Invented spelling: Letters represent sounds heard
  6. Conventional spelling: Standard spelling emerges

Oral Language Development

Kindergarteners are rapidly expanding their oral vocabulary and sentence complexity. Rich conversation, read-alouds, and explicit vocabulary instruction support this growth. Children typically:

  • Understand 20,000-24,000 words
  • Speak in sentences of 5-7 words
  • Tell simple stories with a beginning, middle, and end
  • Follow 2-3 step directions
  • Ask many "why" and "how" questions

High-Frequency Words

Kindergarteners should recognize common sight words that don't follow regular phonics patterns. The Dolch and Fry lists provide research-based word sequences. Focus on 25-50 high-frequency words by year's end.

📝 Ready-to-Use Lessons

Phonological Awareness Lessons

Rhyme Time Circle

Objective: Students will identify and produce rhyming words.

Materials: Picture cards, rhyming books

Procedure:

  1. Read a rhyming book aloud, emphasizing rhyming words
  2. Show picture cards and have students identify rhyming pairs
  3. Play "Thumbs Up/Thumbs Down" - do these words rhyme?
  4. Have students generate rhyming words for a given word

Sound Box Elkonin

Objective: Students will segment words into individual phonemes.

Materials: Sound boxes, counters, picture cards

Procedure:

  1. Model saying a CVC word slowly: "cat" → /c/ /a/ /t/
  2. Push a counter into each box as you say each sound
  3. Have students practice with support
  4. Gradually release responsibility to students

Print Awareness Lessons

Book Walk Detective

Objective: Students will identify parts of a book and print concepts.

Materials: Big books, individual books

Procedure:

  1. Introduce "detective" vocabulary: cover, title, author, illustrator
  2. Model finding each part on a big book
  3. Students practice with partner books
  4. Create a class chart of book parts

Writing Workshop Mini-Lessons

Adding Details to Pictures

Objective: Students will add details to drawings to tell a complete story.

Procedure:

  1. Share a student drawing (with permission)
  2. Ask: "What else could we add to help readers understand?"
  3. Model adding details: setting, characters, expressions
  4. Students return to their own writing to add details

Interactive Read-Aloud Structure

Before Reading:

  • Activate prior knowledge
  • Preview vocabulary
  • Set a purpose for listening

During Reading:

  • Think aloud at strategic points
  • Ask questions to check understanding
  • Have students turn and talk

After Reading:

  • Discuss key events and ideas
  • Make connections
  • Extend through writing or art

🔍 Investigations & Close Reading

Close Reading for Kindergarten

Even young learners can engage in close reading when scaffolded appropriately. Use short, rich texts and read multiple times with different purposes.

Three Reads Strategy for Kindergarten

First Read: Enjoy the story - "What is this text about?"

Second Read: Notice details - "What do you notice about the words/pictures?"

Third Read: Dig deeper - "What does the author want us to learn/feel?"

Text Analysis Activities

Character Investigation

Focus Question: What is [character] like? How do you know?

  • Draw the character
  • Add speech bubbles with things they say
  • Write or dictate character traits
  • Find evidence in pictures and words

Story Elements Hunt

Materials: Story elements anchor chart, sticky notes

Activity:

  1. After reading, give each table group sticky notes
  2. Groups find and mark: characters, setting, problem, solution
  3. Share findings with whole class
  4. Create a class story map

Writing Workshops

Opinion Writing Investigation

Question: Which is the best pet - cat or dog?

  1. Read texts about both animals
  2. Create a T-chart of observations
  3. Students form opinions with reasons
  4. Draw and write/dictate opinion pieces

How-To Writing Study

  1. Read several "how-to" texts together
  2. Notice features: steps, numbers, sequence words
  3. Create a class how-to text
  4. Students write their own how-to books

📊 Assessment Strategies

Formative Assessments

Letter and Sound Assessment

Use alphabet cards to assess:

  • Letter name recognition (upper and lowercase)
  • Letter sound production
  • Letter formation

Benchmark: All 52 letter names and 26 sounds by end of year

Phonological Awareness Screening

  • Rhyme recognition and production
  • Syllable counting and blending
  • Initial sound identification
  • Phoneme segmentation (CVC words)

Running Records

For students reading emergent texts:

  • Track accuracy, self-corrections, and reading behaviors
  • Analyze error patterns
  • Determine instructional reading level

Summative Assessments

  • Attempting letters/words

📚 Vocabulary Instruction

Academic Language for Kindergarten

Essential Literary Terms

  • Author: The person who writes the words
  • Illustrator: The person who draws the pictures
  • Character: A person or animal in the story
  • Setting: Where and when the story happens
  • Beginning/Middle/End: Parts of a story
  • Fiction: Made-up stories
  • Nonfiction: True information

Vocabulary Instruction Strategies

Text Talk Method

  1. Read the word in context
  2. Explain the meaning in child-friendly terms
  3. Give examples beyond the story
  4. Have children repeat the word
  5. Use the word throughout the day

Word of the Day Routine

  1. Introduce word with picture/gesture
  2. Say it, clap syllables, act it out
  3. Use in multiple sentences
  4. Listen for it during read-aloud
  5. Add to word wall with picture

🎯 Differentiation Strategies

Supporting Struggling Readers

Scaffolding Strategies

  • Pre-teach vocabulary before read-alouds
  • Provide picture supports for comprehension
  • Use manipulatives for phonemic awareness
  • Offer additional practice with decodable texts
  • Allow extended wait time for responses
  • Break tasks into smaller steps

English Language Learner (ELL) Support

Strategies for ELL Students

  • Use visual supports and real objects (realia)
  • Pair with bilingual buddy when possible
  • Provide native language text supports
  • Focus on oral language development
  • Use Total Physical Response (TPR)
  • Build vocabulary through repeated exposure
  • Accept approximations and celebrate growth

Cognate Awareness

Many English words have Spanish cognates. Help Spanish-speaking students make connections: family/familia, letter/letra, important/importante

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

Reading Misconceptions

Letter/Sound Confusion

Misconception: Letter names and sounds are the same

Reality: The letter name "B" and the sound /b/ are different

Teaching Tip: Consistently distinguish between "letter name" and "letter sound" in instruction

Memorizing vs. Reading

Misconception: If a child can recite a book, they can read

Reality: Memorization is different from decoding print

Teaching Tip: Celebrate book memory while also teaching print concepts and phonics

Pictures vs. Print

Misconception: Reading comes from the pictures only

Reality: Pictures support comprehension, but meaning comes from words

Teaching Tip: Point to words while reading; show that print carries the message

Writing Misconceptions

Invented Spelling is "Wrong"

Misconception: Children should only write words they spell correctly

Reality: Invented spelling shows phonemic awareness and supports writing development

Teaching Tip: Encourage risk-taking in writing; praise sound-letter connections

Perfect Handwriting First

Misconception: Focus on letter formation before writing messages

Reality: Message-making can develop alongside handwriting

Teaching Tip: Value content while also teaching proper letter formation

Phonological Awareness Misconceptions

Phonics = Phonological Awareness

Misconception: These terms mean the same thing

Reality: Phonological awareness is oral/auditory; phonics connects sounds to letters

Teaching Tip: Practice phonological awareness WITHOUT print, then connect to letters

Common Teaching Error

Skipping phonological awareness and jumping straight to phonics instruction. Children need strong oral sound manipulation skills before they can effectively connect sounds to letters.

🧒 AI Tools for Students

Important Considerations

Kindergarteners should not use AI tools independently. All AI use should be teacher-facilitated, with adult supervision and guidance. Focus on AI as a tool to support learning, not replace instruction.

Digital Citizenship Foundations

Even in kindergarten, begin conversations about:

  • AI is a helpful tool, like a calculator or dictionary
  • People create AI to help us, but it can make mistakes
  • We always check if information makes sense
  • AI doesn't replace our thinking—it helps us think
